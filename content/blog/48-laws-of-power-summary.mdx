Robert Greene spent years studying history's most powerful figures — kings, generals, courtiers, seducers, and con artists — and distilled everything they knew into 48 laws. The result was one of the most controversial and widely read books of the last 30 years.

This is not a book about being evil. It is a book about understanding how power actually works — so you stop being naive about it. Whether you choose to use these laws or simply recognize when others are using them on you, knowing them changes how you see every room you walk into.

Here is the complete list of all 48 laws, explained in plain language with the real story behind each one and how it applies to modern life.

---

## Law 1: Never Outshine the Master

The most dangerous mistake you can make around someone with authority over you is making them feel inferior. Even if you are more talented, more intelligent, or more capable — showing it openly triggers resentment, not admiration. Make your superiors feel like the smartest person in the room. Make your wins feel like their wins.

Nicolas Fouquet, the French finance minister, threw the most spectacular party of the 17th century to impress King Louis XIV. He was arrested three weeks later. His crime was not corruption. It was making the king feel small.

**How to apply it:** Before any interaction with someone above you, ask yourself — am I making this person feel secure or threatened? The answer determines everything that follows.

---

## Law 2: Never Put Too Much Trust in Friends, Learn How to Use Enemies

Friends are dangerous precisely because you trust them. They know your weaknesses, take liberties, and expect preferential treatment. A former enemy brought into your circle has everything to prove — they work harder, stay more loyal, and are less likely to betray you because they know what it cost to earn your trust.

**How to apply it:** Keep your friendships separate from your working relationships. The people you like are not always the people you should rely on.

---

## Law 3: Conceal Your Intentions

When people know what you want, they know how to stop you. The most effective players never reveal their true goal until it is already achieved. They misdirect, they create false fronts, they let people focus on the wrong thing while the real move happens quietly elsewhere.

Bismarck was a master of this. He publicly declared one intention while privately building something completely different. By the time his opponents understood what he was actually doing, it was already done.

**How to apply it:** Share your process freely. Conceal your destination entirely.

---

## Law 4: Always Say Less Than Necessary

Powerful people speak sparingly. The less you say, the more weight each word carries. Silence creates discomfort — and people will rush to fill it, often revealing things they never intended to share. The more you talk, the more likely you are to say something that weakens your position.

**How to apply it:** In your next important conversation, make your point and then stop. Resist every urge to elaborate. Watch what the other person does with the silence.

---

## Law 5: So Much Depends on Reputation — Guard It with Your Life

Your reputation precedes you into every room. It shapes what people expect from you before you say a word. A strong reputation is a force multiplier — it intimidates enemies, attracts allies, and creates opportunities. A damaged reputation takes years to rebuild. Know what you want to be known for and protect it aggressively.

**How to apply it:** Google yourself. Ask someone you trust what people say about you when you are not there. If the answer surprises you, you have work to do.

---

## Law 6: Court Attention at All Costs

In a world full of noise, the invisible have no power. Attention is the prerequisite for everything else — influence, opportunity, advancement. Being modest and self-effacing might feel safe, but in the long run it guarantees obscurity. Find what makes you genuinely different and amplify it deliberately.

**How to apply it:** What is the one thing about you that nobody else in your field does or is? If you cannot answer that quickly, that is the problem.

---

## Law 7: Get Others to Do the Work for You, but Always Take the Credit

The game of power rewards those who are associated with results, not those who quietly produce them. Build systems. Leverage other people's effort. Position yourself at the center of valuable work so that credit flows to you naturally. This is not theft — it is architecture.

Edison did not invent most of what he is credited with. He built the lab, hired the talent, and made sure every breakthrough carried his name. Tesla was the more brilliant scientist. Edison understood the credit game better.

**How to apply it:** Stop spending your best hours on tasks someone else could do. Your highest value is in thinking, deciding, and connecting — not executing.

---

## Law 8: Make Other People Come to You — Use Bait if Necessary

When you make the first move toward someone, you give them information about how much you want or need them. The person who waits, who creates the conditions that make others come to them, holds the superior position. Lure people in rather than chasing them.

**How to apply it:** In negotiations, let the other side name the first number. In relationships, let them initiate. Whoever moves first reveals the most.

---

## Law 9: Win Through Your Actions, Never Through Argument

Winning an argument is almost always a Pyrrhic victory. Even if you are right, you make the other person feel humiliated — and humiliated people do not forget. Demonstrate your point through action and let the results speak. A person convinced against their will is of the same opinion still.

**How to apply it:** Next time you feel the urge to prove someone wrong in a meeting, do not argue. Do it. Show the result. Let the outcome make your case.

---

## Law 10: Infection — Avoid the Unhappy and Unlucky

Misery is contagious. People who are chronically unfortunate, negative, or chaotic will drag you into their orbit before you realize what is happening. This is not about being cold — it is about protecting your energy, your reputation, and your trajectory. The people around you become your ceiling.

**How to apply it:** Look at your five closest relationships. Are they pulling you up or holding you in place?

---

## Law 11: Learn to Keep People Dependent on You

The most secure position in any organization or relationship is indispensability. If people can function perfectly well without you, you are replaceable. If you are the only person who knows something, can do something, or has access to something — you are protected.

**How to apply it:** Identify the one thing only you do in your current role. Deepen that skill until it becomes irreplaceable.

---

## Law 12: Use Selective Honesty and Generosity to Disarm Your Victim

A single genuine act of openness or generosity can lower someone's defenses completely. People are so accustomed to hidden agendas that a moment of real honesty — strategically placed — creates enormous trust. Use this to disarm people who might otherwise be suspicious of you.

**How to apply it:** When you need someone's cooperation, lead with a genuine concession or acknowledgment before making your ask.

---

## Law 13: When Asking for Help, Appeal to People's Self-Interest, Never to Their Mercy or Gratitude

Nobody helps you because you need help. They help you because helping you benefits them in some way — it makes them feel good, it serves their goals, or it earns them something. Frame every request around what the other person gains, not what you need.

**How to apply it:** Before asking for anything, spend two minutes thinking about what is in it for the other person. Lead with that.

---

## Law 14: Pose as a Friend, Work as a Spy

Information is power. The more you know about someone's true desires, fears, and motivations, the more effectively you can work with — or around — them. Social situations are intelligence gathering opportunities. Ask questions, listen more than you speak, and pay attention to what people reveal when their guard is down.

**How to apply it:** In every conversation, listen for what the other person most wants and most fears. They will almost always tell you if you ask the right questions.

---

## Law 15: Crush Your Enemy Totally

A defeated enemy who is left with resources, resentment, and time will eventually come back. Partial victories create future problems. When you must win, win completely. Leave no ember burning.

**How to apply it:** In professional conflicts — when you have to win, make sure the issue is resolved permanently, not just temporarily defused.

---

## Law 16: Use Absence to Increase Respect and Honor

Presence creates familiarity. Familiarity breeds contempt. The person who is always available, always visible, always accessible loses their mystique. Strategic absence — disappearing at the right moment — creates scarcity, and scarcity increases perceived value.

**How to apply it:** Stop being so available. Let people wonder about you occasionally. The slight anxiety of your absence reminds them of your value.

---

## Law 17: Keep Others in Suspended Terror — Cultivate an Air of Unpredictability

Predictable people are easy to manage, easy to dismiss, and easy to counter. When your behavior is occasionally unpredictable, people pay more attention. They cannot settle into a comfortable routine around you. Uncertainty keeps people slightly off-balance — which keeps them focused on you.

**How to apply it:** Break one pattern this week. Do something nobody expected from you. Observe the response.

---

## Law 18: Do Not Build Fortresses to Protect Yourself — Isolation Is Dangerous

Retreating from the world feels safe but creates vulnerability. Isolation cuts you off from information, from allies, and from the shifting currents of power. You need to stay connected to what is happening around you. Walls that keep threats out also keep opportunity out.

**How to apply it:** If you have been withdrawing — from relationships, from your industry, from public life — it is costing you more than you realize.

---

## Law 19: Know Who You're Dealing With — Do Not Offend the Wrong Person

Not everyone responds the same way to the same approach. Some people are pragmatic and move on. Others nurse grievances for years and wait for the moment to retaliate. Before you make a move against someone, understand what kind of person they are. The wrong enemy can consume you entirely.

**How to apply it:** Before any significant confrontation, ask yourself honestly — what kind of person am I dealing with? Do they let things go or do they hold on?

---

## Law 20: Do Not Commit to Anyone

The moment you align yourself openly with one side, you become a pawn of that side. The most powerful position is the one everyone is trying to court. Stay independent. Let competing factions pursue you rather than pledging yourself to either. Your value drops the moment you commit.

**How to apply it:** In any situation where multiple parties want your support, do not rush to declare loyalty. The longer you wait, the better the terms you can negotiate.

---

## Law 21: Play a Sucker to Catch a Sucker — Seem Dumber Than Your Mark

Intelligence intimidates people. When you appear less capable than you are, you give others a false sense of security that makes them careless. They underestimate you, they lower their guard, they reveal their hand. This is not stupidity — it is strategy.

**How to apply it:** Stop announcing your capabilities. Let people discover them through your results.

---

## Law 22: Use the Surrender Tactic — Transform Weakness into Power

When you are genuinely outmatched, fighting back wastes resources and breeds resentment. Surrender strategically instead. Buy time, preserve your strength, wait for the moment to shift. The person who seems to yield often outlasts the one who fights to the end.

**How to apply it:** There is no shame in stepping back from a fight you cannot win today. The goal is to be positioned correctly when the situation changes.

---

## Law 23: Concentrate Your Forces

Scattered effort produces scattered results. The person who focuses intensely on one thing, one skill, one goal, or one powerful patron consistently outperforms the person who spreads their energy across many fronts. Depth beats breadth in almost every domain.

**How to apply it:** What are you spending energy on right now that is diluting your focus on the thing that actually matters?

---

## Law 24: Play the Perfect Courtier

Power in any organization is partly about results and partly about how you navigate the social environment around those results. The courtier — the person who is politically intelligent, who knows how to make superiors feel good and rivals feel respected — survives and advances. Raw talent without social intelligence stalls.

**How to apply it:** Study the informal power structures in your workplace. Who actually has influence? Who are the key relationships? This map is as important as your job description.

---

## Law 25: Re-Create Yourself

Do not accept the identity that circumstances or other people have assigned to you. The most powerful people in history have consciously designed their own personas — they decided who they would be and projected that image consistently until it became reality. You have more control over your identity than you think.

**How to apply it:** If you could design the version of yourself that the world sees from scratch — how would that person carry themselves, speak, dress, and operate? Start closing the gap between that and your current reality.

---

## Law 26: Keep Your Hands Clean

Power requires plausible deniability. The most effective players ensure that when something goes wrong, they are not visibly connected to it. Use intermediaries. Maintain your reputation for fairness and goodwill even when making difficult moves. The person with clean hands can operate with far more freedom.

**How to apply it:** When you need to deliver bad news, make a difficult cut, or execute an unpopular decision — think carefully about how you structure it so your core reputation is protected.

---

## Law 27: Play on People's Need to Believe to Create a Cultlike Following

People desperately want to believe in something — a cause, a vision, a leader, an idea. The person who offers a compelling vision and backs it with certainty and energy will attract followers. This is how movements, cults, companies, and religions are built. Offer the dream. Vagueness about the details is a feature, not a bug.

**How to apply it:** What is the vision you are offering the people around you? If you cannot state it compellingly in two sentences, you are not leading — you are just managing.

---

## Law 28: Enter Action with Boldness

Timid requests get timid results. Hesitation signals doubt — and doubt makes others doubt you too. When you move, move with complete commitment. The bold person intimidates. The hesitant person invites negotiation and resistance.

**How to apply it:** Think of one thing you have been approaching tentatively. What would it look like to go at it with full commitment instead?

---

## Law 29: Plan All the Way to the End

Most people plan their first few moves and then improvise. The most effective players think all the way to the desired outcome, anticipate the obstacles that will appear along the way, and plan their response in advance. Being surprised is almost always a failure of planning.

**How to apply it:** Before starting anything significant, spend time on the question — what are the three most likely ways this goes wrong, and what is my response to each?

---

## Law 30: Make Your Accomplishments Seem Effortless

Effort is unattractive. When you visibly struggle, you invite doubt about your capability. The person who produces great results while appearing calm and natural seems like a natural talent — and natural talent is far more impressive than hard work. Conceal the labor. Present only the result.

**How to apply it:** Stop talking about how hard you are working. Let the output speak. The gap between effort visible and results produced is where mystique lives.

---

## Law 31: Control the Options — Get Others to Play with the Cards You Deal

True control means shaping the choices available to others, not just influencing which option they pick. When you define the menu, any choice they make serves your interests. The person who controls the frame of a decision controls the outcome of it.

**How to apply it:** When you want someone to agree to something, do not ask open-ended questions. Offer two options — both of which work for you.

---

## Law 32: Play to People's Fantasies

The real world is hard and disappointing. The person who offers an escape from that — who sells a vision of how things could be rather than how they are — will always attract followers. People do not want truth as much as they want hope. Give them the dream and they will forgive almost anything else.

**How to apply it:** What is the fantasy your audience, your customers, or your team wants to believe? Are you speaking to that directly?

---

## Law 33: Discover Each Man's Thumbscrew

Everyone has a weakness — a need, a fear, an insecurity, a desire — that, once identified, gives you enormous leverage. It might be vanity, greed, loneliness, the need for approval, or the fear of being exposed. Find it by watching and listening carefully. The person who knows other people's pressure points can move them without force.

**How to apply it:** In your most important relationships, what does the other person most want and most fear? How much of your interaction is shaped by understanding that?

---

## Law 34: Be Royal in Your Own Fashion — Act Like a King to Be Treated Like One

How you carry yourself determines how others treat you. If you act like you expect to be taken seriously, people take you seriously. If you act uncertain of your own worth, people sense it and treat you accordingly. Self-belief is not arrogance — it is a signal that shapes how every interaction unfolds.

**How to apply it:** How would the most confident version of you walk into a room? Sit in a meeting? Ask for something? Start closing the gap between that and what you actually do.

---

## Law 35: Master the Art of Timing

The right action at the wrong time fails. The same action at the right time succeeds effortlessly. Reading the rhythm of events — knowing when to push and when to wait, when to speak and when to be silent, when to act and when to let things develop — is one of the rarest and most valuable skills in the game of power.

**How to apply it:** Before making an important move, ask — is now the right moment, or would this land better if I waited?

---

## Law 36: Disdain Things You Cannot Have — Ignoring Them Is the Best Revenge

When you visibly want something you cannot have, you advertise your frustration and give power to the thing you cannot reach. Pretending you never wanted it — treating it as beneath your attention — is the most effective response. Desire denied is only painful if you acknowledge the denial.

**How to apply it:** Think of something you have been frustrated about not having. What would it look like to genuinely stop caring about it?

---

## Law 37: Create Compelling Spectacles

People believe what they see more than what they hear. The leader who creates powerful visual symbols, rituals, and displays commands attention and loyalty in a way that words alone never can. Symbols bypass rational thinking and speak directly to emotion.

**How to apply it:** In your presentations, your personal brand, or your leadership style — where could you create a more powerful visual impression?

---

## Law 38: Think as You Like but Behave Like Others

Expressing unconventional ideas openly invites unnecessary conflict. You can hold any belief you want — but publicly deviating too far from social norms makes you a target. Blend in on the surface. Reserve your true thinking for the rare people who can handle it and the right moments to deploy it.

**How to apply it:** Pick your battles with conventional thinking carefully. Not every unorthodox idea needs to be announced.

---

## Law 39: Stir Up Waters to Catch Fish

Emotional agitation clouds judgment. The person who can remain calm while provoking others into anger, impatience, or frustration holds a significant advantage. Angry people make mistakes. Calm people exploit those mistakes.

**How to apply it:** In any tense situation, the person who stays calm longest wins. Practice not reacting to provocation.

---

## Law 40: Despise the Free Lunch

Everything has a price. Gifts create obligations. Favors generate debts. Apparent generosity often conceals hooks. The person who pays their own way remains independent. The person who accepts too much from others eventually finds themselves owing things they did not agree to.

**How to apply it:** Be suspicious of generosity that comes without an obvious reason. What does this person expect in return?

---

## Law 41: Avoid Stepping into a Great Man's Shoes

Following a legend is a losing position. You will be constantly compared to your predecessor and always found wanting, because people remember the past more fondly than it deserves. If you must follow someone great, differentiate completely — do not try to replicate what they did. Forge an entirely new identity.

**How to apply it:** If you are in a role previously held by someone celebrated, stop trying to be them. Find the gap they left and fill that instead.

---

## Law 42: Strike the Shepherd and the Sheep Will Scatter

Most group conflicts are driven by one instigator. Groups rarely act on their own — they need someone to organize and inflame them. Identify that person and address them directly rather than trying to manage the group. Remove the center and the periphery dissolves.

**How to apply it:** When facing organized resistance, look for who is actually driving it. Engaging with everyone wastes your energy.

---

## Law 43: Work on the Hearts and Minds of Others

Coercion produces compliance but not loyalty. The person who wins hearts as well as minds — who makes people genuinely want to follow — builds something far more durable than the person who relies on fear or money alone. Emotion is the real lever of lasting influence.

**How to apply it:** Think about the people you most need on your side. Are you appealing to their logic, their interests, or their emotions? You need all three.

---

## Law 44: Disarm and Infuriate with the Mirror Effect

Mirroring someone — reflecting their behavior, their values, their self-image back at them — creates a deeply disorienting effect. It forces them to see themselves clearly for the first time. Used strategically, it can neutralize aggression, expose hypocrisy, and create a bond that feels almost magical.

**How to apply it:** In difficult conversations, try reflecting the other person's language and framing back at them before introducing your own position. Watch what happens.

---

## Law 45: Preach the Need for Change, but Never Reform Too Much at Once

People love the idea of change but resist the reality of it. Too much change too quickly triggers fear and backlash, even when the change is obviously beneficial. Introduce change gradually, dress it in the language of continuity, and let people feel like they are evolving rather than being disrupted.

**How to apply it:** When implementing something new, frame it as a natural extension of what already works rather than a replacement for it.

---

## Law 46: Never Appear Too Perfect

Flawlessness breeds envy and envy creates silent enemies. Showing strategic vulnerability — admitting a minor weakness, laughing at yourself, acknowledging a mistake — makes you human and disarms the resentment that perfection generates. Nobody roots for someone who appears to have it all figured out.

**How to apply it:** Identify one genuine weakness you can acknowledge publicly that makes you more relatable without actually undermining your authority.

---

## Law 47: Do Not Go Past the Mark You Aimed For — In Victory, Learn When to Stop

Overreach destroys more careers than failure does. The intoxication of winning makes people push further than they should, triggering resistance they had no need to create. Know what you came for. Take it. Leave.

**How to apply it:** Define what winning looks like before you start any significant endeavor. When you hit that mark, stop — even if you feel you could get more.

---

## Law 48: Assume Formlessness

The final law is the master law. Fixed strategies are predictable. Predictable people are easy to defeat. The most powerful players adapt constantly — they have no permanent form, no fixed approach, no identity that can be pinned down and countered. Be water. Shape yourself to every situation.

**How to apply it:** Where in your life are you being too rigid — too committed to one approach, one identity, or one strategy? What would it look like to become more fluid?

## What to Do with These Laws

Reading a list like this is the easy part. The hard part — the part that almost nobody does — is actually applying even one of them consistently.

Most people read this book, feel a rush of recognition, and then go back to doing exactly what they were doing before. The knowledge fades. The behaviors do not change.

That is the real problem with self-help books. Not the content. The format.

If you want to actually absorb these laws and start living them — not just knowing them — the way to do it is one law at a time, with real reflection on how each one applies to your specific life right now.

That is exactly what Unmention is built for. Instead of reading all 48 laws in one sitting and forgetting most of them, you work through 7 laws a day over 7 days — with reflection questions after each one, specific commitments you write down, and follow-ups that hold you accountable to what you said you would do.

The knowledge does not stay on the page. It becomes part of how you think.
